The Mind as Mountain

Zen Practice: Enlightenment as seeing the ordinary

The Three Mountains

Zen offers perhaps the most radical horizontalization of enlightenment. Consider the famous teaching:

"Before I studied Zen, mountains were mountains, and waters were waters.
After I studied Zen for some time, mountains were no longer mountains, and waters were no longer waters.
But now, after understanding Zen, mountains are again mountains, and waters are again waters."


This isn't mystical obfuscation. It's a precise map of the mind's journey from ordinary consciousness, through philosophical confusion, back to ordinary consciousness—but transformed.

The Three Stages in Detail

Each stage represents a different relationship with reality.

Stage 1: Ordinary Mind

  • Relationship: Unquestioned identification
  • Mountain is: Just a mountain
  • State: Naive realism
  • Problem: Takes appearance for reality
  • Metaphor: Sleepwalking

Stage 2: Philosophical Mind

  • Relationship: Analytical distance
  • Mountain is: A concept, an illusion
  • State: Deconstruction
  • Problem: Gets lost in abstraction
  • Metaphor: Lost in the map

Stage 3: Zen Mind

  • Relationship: Direct participation
  • Mountain is: Fully a mountain
  • State: Suchness (Tathata)
  • Achievement: Freedom from both naivete and analysis
  • Metaphor: Awake to what is

The Western mind often gets stuck at Stage 2: perpetual analysis, deconstruction, questioning. Zen's goal is Stage 3: returning to the ordinary with clear eyes.

Zazen: Just Sitting

Zazen meditation is the core Zen practice. It's famously simple and famously difficult.

Posture as Mind

The physical posture creates the mental state. Straight spine, relaxed shoulders, hands in cosmic mudra. You don't achieve enlightenment—you sit in the posture of enlightenment.

Following the Breath

Attention on the natural breath. Not controlling it, just noticing it. When mind wanders (it will), gently return to breath. This is the entire practice.

No Goal, No Achievement

The paradox: You sit without seeking enlightenment. Seeking is the obstacle. Just sitting is itself enlightenment.

Thoughts as Weather

Thoughts arise like clouds. Don't fight them, don't follow them. Let them pass. The sky (awareness) remains unchanged.

Zazen isn't about achieving special states. It's about being completely ordinary. The most boring, simple, straightforward activity: sitting and breathing.

Koans: Short-Circuiting the Analytical Mind

Koans are paradoxical questions designed to break habitual thinking.

1

The Sound of One Hand

"What is the sound of one hand clapping?" This isn't a riddle with an answer. It's a tool to exhaust the analytical mind so something else can emerge.

2

Original Face

"What was your original face before your parents were born?" The question points to the awareness that precedes identity, the ground of being itself.

3

Joshu's Dog

A monk asks: "Does a dog have Buddha-nature?" Joshu replies: "Mu!" (No/without). The answer isn't intellectual—it's an experience of the question itself.

4

The Purpose

Koans aren't solved. They're dissolved. When the mind stops trying to figure them out, reality presents itself directly.

Zen in Daily Life

Zen isn't confined to the meditation cushion. It's a way of engaging everything.

Activity Ordinary Mind Zen Mind
Washing Dishes Chore to finish quickly, mind elsewhere Just washing dishes: feel of water, sound, movement
Eating Multitasking, barely tasting Just eating: textures, flavors, the miracle of nourishment
Walking Getting from A to B, lost in thought Just walking: feel of ground, rhythm of steps, surroundings
Working Future-focused, stressed about outcomes Just working: the task itself, one action at a time
Conversing Planning response, judging, analyzing Just listening: the person's presence, words as they are

The Zen Maxim

"When walking, just walk. When sitting, just sit. When eating, just eat."

The radical simplicity: Do one thing completely. Be where you are completely. This is enlightenment.

Zen as Sovereignty Training

In a godless world, Zen offers a path to sovereignty through presence.

The Four Foundations of Zen Sovereignty

1. Attention as Territory: Where your attention is, there you are. Zen trains you to inhabit your attention fully.

2. Simplicity as Power: Complexity is distraction. Simplicity is clarity. Doing less, but completely.

3. Acceptance as Freedom: Not passive resignation, but clear seeing of what is. Fighting reality is exhausting; seeing it clearly is liberating.

4. Impermanence as Ground: Everything changes. Trying to hold on creates suffering. Flowing with change creates freedom.

Conclusion: The Mountain of Mind

Your mind is the mountain. Thoughts are the weather passing over it.

The goal isn't to stop the weather (impossible).
The goal isn't to identify with the weather (confusion).
The goal is to be the mountain: solid, present, unchanged by whatever passes.

Enlightenment is Ordinary

Not a special state, but clear perception of the ordinary. The mountain being fully a mountain.

Practice is Life

Not preparation for something else. Sitting zazen is itself enlightenment. Washing dishes is itself enlightenment.

Suffering is Resistance

Pain is inevitable. Suffering is resistance to what is. Zen practice reduces the resistance.

Starting Practice

1. Sit daily: 5 minutes. Just sit. Straight spine, natural breath. When mind wanders, return to breath.

2. Choose one activity: Make it "just this" practice. Washing dishes, walking, drinking tea. Do it with full attention.

3. Notice the mountain: Throughout day, check: Am I the mountain (awareness) or the weather (thoughts)?

4. Embrace boredom: When bored, don't reach for distraction. Sit with it. Boredom is the mind craving stimulation. Let it be.
