The Long Now

Thinking in centuries, not quarters

The Temporal Horizon Problem

We've cultivated patient attention. Now we extend it further—beyond our lifetime, beyond our children's lifetime, into what the Chinese call "the long now."

Modern civilization operates on increasingly short time horizons:
• Corporations: Quarterly earnings
• Politics: Election cycles (2-6 years)
• Media: 24-hour news cycles
• Individuals: Monthly bills, weekly goals

This creates a cognitive blind spot: we're optimizing for short-term results at the expense of long-term survival. The slow burn requires expanding your temporal horizon to match the scale of what you're building.

The "Long Now" isn't just about patience. It's about a fundamental shift in identity: from seeing yourself as an individual with a brief lifespan to seeing yourself as a link in a chain that stretches centuries in both directions.


This chapter explores multi-generational thinking. How to make decisions from your great-grandchildren's perspective. What you would build if it needed to last 300 years. And why thinking in centuries is the ultimate antidote to the frantic short-termism of modern life.

The Three Temporal Modes

Different scales of thinking produce different kinds of decisions, different kinds of work, different kinds of lives.

Short-Term Thinking (Default)

  • Timeframe: Days to years
  • Identity: Individual self
  • Question: "What benefits me now?"
  • Values: Speed, efficiency, growth
  • Architecture: Disposable, modular
  • Risk Perception: Immediate threats
  • Examples: Quarterly reports, viral content

Medium-Term Thinking (Rare)

  • Timeframe: Decades
    • Identity: Family, organization
  • Question: "What benefits my children?"
  • Values: Stability, quality, legacy
  • Architecture: Durable, maintainable
  • Risk Perception: Systemic risks
  • Examples: University endowments, family businesses

Long-Term Thinking (Ancient)

  • Timeframe: Centuries to millennia
  • Identity: Civilization, species
  • Question: "What benefits humanity in 300 years?"
  • Values: Permanence, wisdom, stewardship
  • Architecture: Timeless, self-maintaining
  • Risk Perception: Existential risks
  • Examples: Cathedrals, sequoia planting, language preservation

The Scale Determines the Substance

Thinking at different scales isn't just thinking about the same things for longer periods. It's thinking about different things entirely. Short-term thinking concerns itself with optimization. Long-term thinking concerns itself with foundations. They're different domains with different rules, different values, different measures of success.

Principles of Long-Term Thinking

Long-term thinking follows different rules than short-term optimization. These principles guide decisions at century-scale.

1. The Lindy Principle

The Rule: The future life expectancy of something is proportional to its current age. What has lasted is likely to last.
Application: Favor ideas, tools, and practices that have stood the test of time. Avoid novelty for novelty's sake. If something has been useful for 100 years, it will likely be useful for 100 more. If it's 10 years old, be skeptical.
Example: The book has lasted 500+ years. The tweet has lasted 15. Which format should you invest in for lasting impact?

2. The Multi-Generational Test

The Rule: Evaluate decisions by asking: "Would this make sense to my great-grandchildren?"
Application: This filters out short-term optimization. Things that make sense in quarterly reports often look foolish across generations. Things that seem inefficient now often look wise across centuries.
Example: Clear-cutting forests for quick profit fails the test. Planting oak trees you'll never see mature passes it.

3. The Cathedral Principle

The Rule: Some projects require multiple generations to complete. Start them anyway.
Application: Work on things you won't live to see finished. This changes your relationship to the work: you're not creating a final product, you're creating a foundation for others to build upon.
Example: Medieval cathedral builders knew they wouldn't see completion. They worked for the glory of God and future generations, not personal recognition.

4. The Seventh Generation Principle

The Rule: From Iroquois philosophy: consider the impact of decisions on the seventh generation to come.
Application: This expands responsibility beyond immediate stakeholders. It forces consideration of externalities, of systems, of unintended consequences that compound over time.
Example: Industrial practices that pollute for profit fail this test. Sustainable practices that preserve resources for future generations pass it.

The Anti-Fragility of Long Timeframes

Short-term thinking creates fragile systems optimized for specific conditions. Long-term thinking creates anti-fragile systems that can adapt to changing conditions. What works for centuries isn't optimized for any particular moment—it's robust across many moments. This is why institutions that think in centuries often outlast those that think in quarters.

Practical Applications: Living in the Long Now

How do you incorporate long-term thinking into daily life and work?

1

The 100-Year Project

The Practice: Start a project designed to take 100 years to complete. It could be:
• A family history compiled across generations
• A garden with trees that will mature in 80 years
• A knowledge repository added to by multiple people
Why It Works: This physically anchors you to long-term thinking. You make decisions differently when you know you're starting something you won't finish. It also creates something that connects generations.

2

The Future Letter

The Practice: Write a letter to someone 100 years in the future. Explain your world, your values, your hopes. Ask questions about their world. Store it to be opened in 2124.
Why It Works: This exercises the imagination of long-term consequences. Writing to a specific future person makes abstract long-term thinking concrete. It also creates a time capsule of thought that might be valuable to future generations.

3

The Legacy Audit

The Practice: Quarterly, review your work and ask:
1. What will this mean in 10 years?
2. What will this mean in 50 years?
3. What will this mean in 100 years?
4. Would my great-grandchildren be proud of this?
Why It Works: This creates a feedback loop for long-term thinking. Most decisions are evaluated immediately. Adding long-term evaluation surfaces different considerations. It gradually retrains your decision-making toward longer horizons.

4

The Multi-Generational Mentorship

The Practice: Build relationships with people much older and much younger than you. Learn from those who remember different times. Teach those who will live in different times.
Why It Works: This expands your temporal perspective through relationships. Older people provide wisdom about what lasts. Younger people provide insight about what's coming. Together, they stretch your sense of time.

Long-Term Thinking Traps

The Perfection Paralysis

Never starting because you're trying to create something perfect for eternity. Nothing built by humans is eternal. Long-term thinking is about creating something good enough to last, not perfect forever. Cathedrals have been modified for centuries.

The Disconnect from Present

Becoming so focused on the distant future that you neglect the present. Long-term thinking should inform present action, not replace it. The future is built through present choices, not future fantasies.

The Arrogance of Prediction

Assuming you can predict what will be valuable centuries from now. The key insight of long-term thinking isn't prediction—it's creating things robust enough to be valuable across many possible futures. Focus on durable principles, not specific predictions.

Long-Term vs Short-Term: Divergent Outcomes

The same action produces different results at different time scales.

Action Short-Term Outcome (1-5 years) Long-Term Outcome (50-100 years)
Write daily in a journal Minor improvement in writing skills Comprehensive record of a life, potentially valuable historical document
Plant an oak tree Small tree requiring care, no shade yet Mature tree providing shade, habitat, beauty for generations
Learn a dead language Seems impractical, no immediate use Access to centuries of wisdom, preservation of cultural heritage
Build with quality materials More expensive, slower than cheap alternatives Structure lasts centuries, becomes historical artifact
Teach one person deeply Inefficient compared to mass teaching Knowledge passes through generations, creates lineage
Preserve rather than consume Less immediate satisfaction Resources available for future generations

The Time Preference Reversal

Many actions that seem irrational in the short term become rational in the long term. Conversely, many actions that seem rational in the short term (maximizing immediate gain) become irrational in the long term (destroying future possibilities). Long-term thinking isn't about being more patient—it's about playing a different game with different rules and different payoffs.

Case Studies: Architects of the Long Now

These individuals and institutions thought in timeframes that transcend individual lifespans.

The Medici Family

The Visible: Renaissance banking dynasty.
The Long Now Reality: Thought in 100-year cycles for family wealth and influence. Their patronage wasn't charity—it was multi-generational investment in cultural capital. They funded artists whose work would define their era, creating a legacy that outlasted their financial power.
The Lesson: Their most lasting impact wasn't their banking innovations (which faded) but their cultural investments (which endure). They understood that true legacy operates on different timescales than business success.

John Harrison

The Visible: Inventor of the marine chronometer.
The Long Now Reality: Spent 31 years perfecting his fourth chronometer (H4). Worked on a problem (longitude) that had stumped scientists for centuries. Knew his work would enable safer global navigation for generations.
The Lesson: He worked on a timescale matching the problem. Navigation errors cost lives over centuries; his solution needed to work reliably over centuries. The multi-decade development timeline wasn't inefficiency—it was necessity.

The Long Now Foundation

The Visible: Organization building a 10,000-year clock.
The Long Now Reality: Literally building a clock designed to tell time for 10,000 years. Using materials and designs that will last millennia. Creating an artifact that forces people to think in longer timeframes.
The Lesson: Sometimes the most effective way to change thinking is to build physical artifacts that embody the change. The clock isn't just a timepiece—it's a philosophical argument made of bronze and stone.

The Common Thread: Transcending Self

In each case, success required thinking beyond individual lifespan, beyond immediate returns, beyond personal recognition. The work became meaningful precisely because it transcended the individual. The longer the timeframe, the more the work becomes about contribution rather than consumption, about legacy rather than success.

Conclusion: Your Place in Time

Thinking in the long now changes your relationship to everything: work, relationships, values, identity.

You Are a Transition

You're not an isolated individual. You're a transition between what came before and what comes after. Your choices create the conditions for future generations. Long-term thinking makes this reality conscious rather than accidental.

Small Actions Compound Across Time

At century scale, small consistent actions create massive results. A family tradition maintained for 100 years becomes culture. A small investment compounded for 100 years becomes wealth. A principle upheld for 100 years becomes wisdom. The long now reveals the power of consistency across generations.

The Ultimate Anti-Fragility

Thinking in centuries is the ultimate anti-fragile mindset. It prepares you for volatility by focusing on what endures across volatility. It finds stability not in resisting change but in embracing timescales where change is the constant. What matters at century scale isn't surviving this year's crisis—it's building systems that survive all crises.
